Relative Positioning

So naturally, for our linear gradient we need to grab the Z vector, as we want our gradient to face upward in our material. Let‚Äôs add a component mask before the clamp and use B (Z), unchecking R and G. Once we place this on the mesh, drag the mesh around and you‚Äôll find that when the mesh passes below the grid in editor, Z: 0.0, the mesh will turn black.

We‚Äôll subtract this from our World Position Now you‚Äôll find a hard split of black and white that stays with our mesh when we move it up and down! This will become the basis of our gradient.

Relative Scale

Let‚Äôs bring Object Radius into the mix. Object Radius gives us a value equal to the overall radius of our object. We will subtract this from our object position, you can think of this as a ‚Äòrange‚Äô between bounds and center of the mesh.

Again, we want to make sure our math is subtracted from the world position.

When we do this, you‚Äôll find the mesh is white, this is because we‚Äôre not taking into account the height extent we want our gradient to travel up. We can do this by dividing by a scalar value before our component mask. Changing this value extends or decreases the falloff on our gradient.

Position Offset

Alright! So we now have a very cool looking gradient that we can use for our stylized characters and props. But let‚Äôs say you want the gradient to begin higher up on the mesh instead of at the bottom? Well we know that the Object Position, that gives us the center of the bounds to our object, subtracting the object radius giving us our radius value for the gradient. So why not offset the Z by adding to it? Let‚Äôs give it a shot. To create our new vector, we‚Äôll take a Vector 2 and append on a scalar parameter. This gives us a new vector where X and Y are constant at 0, and the Z is our variable.

If we add this to our math to find the gradient, we retain our X and Y, because adding 0 gives you the same value, and adding the Z‚Äôs together gives us our offset for where the gradient starts.
